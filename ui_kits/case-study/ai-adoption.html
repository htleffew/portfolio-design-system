The AI productivity J-curve, as a thinking tool.

A stylized two-function model of AI adoption, one decaying integration cost and one compounding integration return, wired into an interactive sandbox so the reader can watch the trough deepen, the breakeven slip, and the terminal value swing under three coefficients. The math is illustrative. The exponents were chosen for shape, not estimated from data. The page is a teaching tool for a single structural intuition: the depth of the trough is the variable that decides whether the curve gets to compound at all.

Model Net(t) = Gain(t) - Cost(t), with Gain(t) = a*t^1.85 and Cost(t) = b*t*e^(-t/4.5), parameterized by three coefficients the reader controls
Sandbox Three sliders for legacy debt, talent density, and integration scope, driving a 24-month curve, three KPIs (max drawdown, breakeven horizon, terminal net), and an SVG plot updated on input
Claim One: at fixed integration scope, the depth of the early-month trough is set almost entirely by the ratio of legacy debt to talent density, and the trough sets whether the project survives long enough to compound
01 / Setup

A teaching tool, not an estimate.

What follows is a thinking tool for one structural intuition about enterprise AI adoption: the early-month trough is not a side effect of the curve, the trough is the variable that decides whether the curve compounds at all. The page builds that intuition with a stylized two-function model1Stylized in the technical sense: the functional forms t^1.85 for return and t * e^(-t/4.5) for cost were chosen because they produce the J shape and the early peak the literature on technology-adoption curves describes (Brynjolfsson, Rock & Syverson, 2017). The exponents were not estimated from data. Three coefficients (legacy debt, talent density, integration scope) scale the model; the reader controls them with sliders., an interactive sandbox that lets the reader vary three coefficients, and three downstream readouts the sandbox computes from the curve. There is no empirical claim. The page does not estimate a real organization's ROI; it teaches the shape of the tradeoff that any such estimate has to live inside.

The model has two halves. Cost(t) rises, peaks, and decays, the familiar shape of integration debt: legacy systems that have to be re-routed, security review, talent acquired at a premium, and the organizational overhead of running new pipelines next to old ones. Gain(t) compounds: the longer the system runs, the more processes it touches, and the returns scale super-linearly in integration scope. The net curve is the difference. Early months are dominated by cost; later months are dominated by gain; the depth of the trough between them is the page's central variable.

None of the structural ingredients are novel. The J-curve shape is well-established in the productivity-paradox literature; Brynjolfsson, Rock & Syverson 2017 describe it directly for general-purpose technologies. The contribution, if any, is in making the trade-off interactive: the reader cannot read this page passively. The sandbox forces a hand on the sliders, and the page's claim survives or fails depending on whether the resulting curves match the reader's intuition about the organizations they have actually worked inside.

What the model is for
A stylized model is the wrong tool for forecasting an organization's actual ROI and the right tool for building intuition about which coefficients dominate the answer. The exponents 1.85 and e^(-t/4.5) were chosen to produce a curve that peaks early in cost and accelerates late in gain; they were not fit to any dataset. The sandbox's job is not to tell the reader what their J-curve is, but to let them feel how steeply the trough deepens when integration scope is pushed up and talent density is pulled down.
02 / The two functions

Cost peaks early, gain compounds late.

The model has two halves and one subtraction. Cost(t) rises with the integration burden a new system imposes on the existing organization, peaks during the months when legacy and new systems run in parallel, and decays as the integration completes. Gain(t) is a power-law function of integration scope: deeper integrations into more processes return super-linearly in the time the system is allowed to run, because each additional integrated process compounds the value of the previous ones. The net curve is the difference, and its early-month minimum is the trough that decides whether the project survives the budget cycle.

Fig. 1 , The two functions and their net. Cost peaks at M4-5 then decays as the integration matures. Gain compounds super-linearly in time. The net curve crosses zero at the breakeven horizon, which under the default coefficients lands at M11. The depth of the trough between M0 and the breakeven is the variable the page is about.
Net(t) = Gain(t) - Cost(t)

Gain(t) = (scope * 1.5 * talent * 0.8 / 10) * t^1.85
Cost(t) = (legacy * 25 + (11 - talent) * 15 + scope * 5) * t * e^(-t / 4.5)

Three coefficients enter the curves. Legacy debt scales the cost coefficient linearly. Talent density enters cost as (11 - talent), so a low-talent organization pays the integration cost a high-talent organization avoids; the same coefficient enters gain as a multiplier, so the under-resourced project not only pays more cost but earns less return for the same scope2This double-charge of low talent (more cost, less gain) is the structural reason the model produces such steep troughs at the low-talent edge of the parameter space. It is also, anecdotally, the failure mode reported in the case studies of cancelled enterprise AI integrations: the work that the missing engineers were supposed to do gets billed to the project as overhead.. Integration scope enters cost weakly (a fifth-order linear term) and gain strongly (the multiplier on the power-law term). The asymmetry is deliberate: the model is teaching that scope amplifies the upside far more than the downside, conditional on the talent being there to absorb it.

Why these specific exponents
The exponent 1.85 on the gain term sits between linear (1.0) and quadratic (2.0); it produces the visual feel of a compounding curve that takes most of the 24-month window to dominate. The decay term e^(-t/4.5) on cost peaks at t = 4.5 months, which matches the empirical observation in the technology-adoption literature that integration debt typically peaks in the first half-year. Neither exponent was estimated. Both were chosen to produce the qualitative shape the model is teaching, and the page's argument does not depend on the specific values; it depends on the existence of the trough.
03 / The sandbox

Three sliders, twenty-four months, three readouts.

The sandbox is the part of the page that does the actual work. Three sliders control the coefficients (legacy debt, talent density, integration scope), the SVG plot redraws on input, and three KPIs derive directly from the curve: the maximum drawdown (the deepest point of the trough), the breakeven horizon (the first month after the trough where Net(t) crosses zero again), and the M24 net (the terminal value at the end of the simulated window). The default coefficients (5, 5, 5) produce a moderate trough and a breakeven around M11. Pulling talent down to 1 while pushing scope up to 10 deepens the trough by an order of magnitude and slips the breakeven past M24 entirely.

The sandbox is not a calculator. It does not produce the user's actual breakeven, because the user's actual breakeven depends on coefficients that are not in this model: regulatory exposure, change-management overhead, the political cost of the integration, the rate at which the underlying foundation models are themselves changing. The sandbox produces the qualitative structure those coefficients have to live inside, and the page's argument is that the structure is robust to which specific real-world variables get mapped onto the three sliders.

04 / What the trough teaches

The depth of the trough is the variable that matters.

The argument the sandbox is built to support, in one sentence: the depth of the early-month trough is set almost entirely by the ratio of legacy debt to talent density, and the trough sets whether the project survives long enough to compound. Three coefficients enter the model, but the terminal value at M24 is dominated by the first six months of the curve, because a project that is cancelled at M9 never reaches M24 at all. The page's claim is that this is not a quantitative finding, the exponents are stylized; it is a structural one, the shape is invariant to the specific exponents used as long as cost peaks early and gain compounds late.

Three corollaries follow. The first: increasing integration scope without increasing talent density does not delay the breakeven, it abolishes it; the trough deepens past the threshold at which the project can be defended and the curve never recovers. The second: the value of high-talent integration is not that it makes the gain steeper, it is that it makes the cost shallower, which is the dimension the gain depends on for survival. The third: a low-scope integration with high talent and low legacy debt produces a curve so shallow it can be mistaken for a flat line, which is why isolated chatbot pilots succeed routinely and autonomous-workflow integrations fail routinely. The two cases are not different problems, they are the same J-curve at different scales.

The model is a teaching tool. The coefficients on the sliders are not estimates of any real organization's parameters, and the curve the sandbox produces is not a forecast. What the page commits to is the qualitative shape: that the trough exists, that its depth is determined by legacy-to-talent ratio more than by any other coefficient, and that the depth determines survival in a way the terminal value alone cannot represent. Whether a real organization's integration looks like the curve at (5, 5, 5) or at (8, 2, 7) is not a question the model can answer, but the model can name the question, and naming it correctly is most of the work.

What this page is not
Not an estimate of any organization's actual ROI on AI adoption. Not a benchmark of foundation-model capability or deployment cost. Not a recommendation that integration scope should be high or low; the model is symmetric about that choice and the right answer depends on coefficients the model does not know. The page is a thinking tool, in the same sense Olah's circuits writeups are thinking tools: the math is the medium, the interaction is the lesson, and the reader is expected to come away with a sharper intuition about a tradeoff they already had to make.
