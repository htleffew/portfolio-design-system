Problem framing

Global experiment lift can hide severe local regressions.

Users do not experience products as flat averages. They experience them at intersections of region, hardware, tenure, accessibility behavior, plan type, and task context.

Standard experimentation dashboards optimize for the mythical average user. A release can lift engagement globally, shave latency globally, and still impose sharp friction on a small but meaningful subgroup such as new, solo users in a specific geography on older hardware. Traditional segmentation often misses this because subgroup losses are mathematically washed out by majority gains.

Granular Intersectional Monitoring Platform, or IMP, reframes this problem as an engineering systems issue rather than a one-off analytics drill. Instead of pre-specifying thousands of possible cohorts and diluting statistical power, it starts with observed bad outcomes, then works backward to find overrepresented intersections worth validating.

Foundations

Juggernaut and SCOPEX established two non-negotiables: longitudinal framing and explainability.

The IMP design inherits from two earlier safety programs. Project Juggernaut handled retrospective regulatory analysis at scale. Project SCOPEX translated those findings into a real-time intervention system. Together they forced a move away from reactive dashboards and toward systematic quantitative monitoring.

Project Juggernaut · within-subjects longitudinal analysis

Juggernaut analyzed how users arrived at harmful platform experiences when manual review was impossible. Its core insight was methodological: use each user as their own control. Rather than compare unlike users, split one user’s journey into early, middle, and late tertiles, then measure sentiment drift and diversity narrowing over time.

Project SCOPEX · explainability mandate

SCOPEX moved from retrospective analysis to intervention. It monitored five degradation signals: diversity narrowing, sentiment decline, policy-violation acceleration, violative-content exposure, and negative search behavior. During implementation, a black-box model achieved stronger raw precision, but stakeholders rejected it because they could not inspect why a user was flagged.

Design rule carried into IMP
High-stakes experimentation cannot hide behind predictive accuracy. If product, policy, legal, or safety stakeholders cannot inspect why a subgroup was flagged, model quality is incomplete even when metrics look strong.
Platform architecture

IMP reverses standard segmentation order: outcome first, explanation second.

Rather than guess which user cohorts might be harmed, IMP identifies users with negative outcomes, detects overrepresented intersections among them, validates those intersections statistically, then trains interpretable predictive layers on confirmed patterns. That sequencing is the core architectural move.

Phase 01

Discovery stays narrow by validating only intersections that are empirically overrepresented among bad outcomes.

This is where IMP avoids both combinatorial explosion and statistical theater.

  1. Outcome identification. Compute a composite negative-outcome score from churn, support tickets, high undo-command rates, abandonment, or comparable friction signals.
  2. Intersection analysis. Scan the constrained persona schema for attribute combinations that appear disproportionately among struggling users relative to background distribution.
  3. Targeted validation. Compare treatment versus control inside those candidate intersections with Welch’s t-test, chosen because small subgroup samples rarely support equal-variance assumptions.
  4. Multiple-comparison control. Apply Benjamini-Hochberg to keep the false discovery rate bounded when several candidate intersections survive discovery.

    FDR ≠ raw p
    Without correction, targeted subgroup testing becomes false-positive inflation by another name.
    Ordered p-values satisfy rejection when

    p(i) ≤ (i / m) × α

    where i is rank, m total tests, and α chosen FDR threshold.
    Figure 03Benjamini-Hochberg keeps focused subgroup testing from turning into uncontrolled fishing.
    Python · Welch’s t-test + Benjamini-Hochberg
    # Implementation of Step 3 and 4: Welch's t-test and Benjamini-Hochberg
import pandas as pd
from scipy import stats
from statsmodels.stats.multitest import multipletests

def validate_intersections(candidate_intersections, treatment_data, control_data, fdr_alpha=0.05):
    results = []
    
    # Step 3: Targeted Validation (Welch's t-test)
    for intersection in candidate_intersections:
        treatment_group = treatment_data[treatment_data['segment'] == intersection]['outcome_score']
        control_group = control_data[control_data['segment'] == intersection]['outcome_score']
        
        # Welch's t-test (equal_var=False)
        t_stat, p_val = stats.ttest_ind(treatment_group, control_group, equal_var=False)
        results.append({'intersection': intersection, 'p_value': p_val, 't_stat': t_stat})
        
    df_results = pd.DataFrame(results)
    
    # Step 4: Multiple Comparison Control (Benjamini-Hochberg)
    reject, pvals_corrected, _, _ = multipletests(df_results['p_value'], alpha=fdr_alpha, method='fdr_bh')
    
    df_results['is_significant'] = reject
    df_results['p_value_adj'] = pvals_corrected
    
    return df_results[df_results['is_significant'] == True]
    Phase 02

    Elastic Net keeps early warning interpretable even when features overlap.

    Once intersections are validated, IMP uses them as labels for predictive modeling. Experiments are featurized from intersection attributes and experiment metadata, then scored with an elastic-net logistic classifier. L1 pressure zeros irrelevant interaction terms; L2 pressure stabilizes correlated ones. Class weighting corrects for the fact that harm is rare by design.

    Coefficient path sketch
    As regularization strengthens, noisy interactions collapse toward zero while persistent drivers remain legible enough to explain.

    Python · Elastic Net logistic classifier
    from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.pipeline import Pipeline

def train_predictive_model(X_train, y_train):
    # X_train contains one-hot encoded intersection attributes and metadata
    # y_train contains binary labels of validated harm (1) or normal (0)
    
    pipeline = Pipeline([
        ('scaler', StandardScaler()),
        ('classifier', LogisticRegression(
            penalty='elasticnet',
            l1_ratio=0.5,
            solver='saga',
            class_weight='balanced',
            max_iter=1000
        ))
    ])
    
    pipeline.fit(X_train, y_train)
    return pipeline

    PySpark · accessibility proxy materialization
    import pyspark.sql.functions as F
from pyspark.sql.types import DoubleType
import math

# UDF for Sigmoid function
def sigmoid(x):
    return 1 / (1 + math.exp(-x))
sigmoid_udf = F.udf(sigmoid, DoubleType())

def calculate_accessibility_proxy(df_events):
    # Assume df_events contains z_scores for behaviors and opt_in flags
    df_scored = df_events.withColumn(
        "raw_score",
        (F.col("opt_in_flag") * 4.0) + 
        (F.when(F.col("z_score_keyboard_nav") > 0, F.col("z_score_keyboard_nav") * 1.5).otherwise(0)) +
        (F.when(F.col("z_score_aria_usage") > 0, F.col("z_score_aria_usage") * 2.0).otherwise(0))
    )
    
    # Apply sigmoid to map to 0.0 - 1.0 probability range
    df_probabilities = df_scored.withColumn(
        "accessibility_likelihood", 
        sigmoid_udf(F.col("raw_score"))
    )
    
    # Flag users exceeding the 0.6 threshold
    return df_probabilities.withColumn(
        "is_a11y_proxy", 
        F.when(F.col("accessibility_likelihood") > 0.6, True).otherwise(False)
    )
    Medallion system

    Bronze to Gold pipeline translates raw events into risk registries, plain-language reports, and auditable suppression logic.

    IMP runs on an AWS-native medallion architecture. Bronze stores raw events, session metrics, and experiment assignments in S3. Silver normalizes them into daily joined snapshots with PySpark. Gold executes Python-based intersectional modeling, then emits FDR-adjusted findings, risk registries, and delivery artifacts such as HTML or PDF reports routed to product teams.

    Bronze · Silver · Gold + governance gate
    Governance is not documentation glued on later. Consent gating and k-anonymity suppression live in pipeline execution paths.

    Governance trigger
    k < 200

    Any intersectional cell below 200 users is automatically suppressed to preserve k-anonymity. Persona generation is additionally gated by user-consent flags at the row level. This prevents “helpful” subgroup analytics from becoming a re-identification vector.

    Python · k-anonymity enforcement
    def enforce_k_anonymity(df_intersections, k_threshold=200):
    # Filter out any intersectional group with fewer than 'k' users
    compliant_df = df_intersections[df_intersections['user_count'] >= k_threshold]
    
    # Log suppressed segments for auditing without revealing user data
    suppressed_count = len(df_intersections) - len(compliant_df)
    print(f"Governance Alert: Suppressed {suppressed_count} segments failing k-anonymity (k={k_threshold})")
    
    return compliant_df
    Conclusion

    Inclusive experimentation becomes measurable only when hidden harm is treated as first-class signal.

    Historical use of IMP revealed three recurring blind spots in ordinary A/B practice. First, hidden harm: globally positive experiments that materially damage a small intersectional subgroup. Second, missed opportunity: features that look neutral overall but generate outsized value for a tightly defined overlap of users. Third, invisible impact: model or ranking changes that degrade specific behavior patterns without tripping any platform-wide alarm.

    • Outcome-first discovery protects statistical power by refusing to test every imaginable cohort upfront.
    • Interpretable modeling preserves trust, stakeholder approval, and reproducibility during safety escalation.
    • Proxy-based accessibility monitoring expands equity coverage without invasive assistive-tech fingerprinting.
    • Governance controls belong inside compute pipeline, not in presentation-layer disclaimers.

    That is the underlying claim of granular intersectional monitoring: stop optimizing for a fictional average user and build tooling that can quantify who is losing, why they are losing, and whether the organization can responsibly act on that signal.
