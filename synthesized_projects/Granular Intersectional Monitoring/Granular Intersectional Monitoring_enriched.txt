# Granular Intersectional Monitoring: A Quantitative Framework for Proactive User Harm Detection

When we run product experiments, we typically rely on global metrics. We look at a dashboard, see a statistically significant lift in engagement or a drop in latency, and declare the experiment a success. Sometimes we slice the data by a single dimension, looking at how the feature performed in a specific region or on a specific operating system. 

*[Interactivity Opportunity: A toggle switch labeled "View Mode". When set to "Global", a single upward-trending line chart is displayed. When toggled to "Intersectional", the single line splits into multiple lines, revealing that while the average goes up, one specific line sharply declines.]*

However, real users do not experience products along a single dimension. They exist at complex intersections of attributes, behaviors, and contexts. A product change that looks like a global win might actually be causing severe friction for a specific subgroup, such as new, solo users operating in a specific geographic region with older hardware. Traditional A/B testing frameworks often mask these "hidden harms" because the negative impact on a small intersectional group is washed out by the positive impact on the majority.

*[2D Visualization Description: A stacked bar chart or a mosaic plot showing the total user base. The majority area is shaded to indicate positive impact, while a tiny, deeply nested rectangle (representing the intersection of 'New', 'Solo', 'Specific Region', 'Old Hardware') is shaded to indicate negative impact. The visualization highlights how easily the small section is mathematically overwhelmed by the larger sections.]*

If we want to build truly equitable products, we need a way to detect these intersectional effects quantitatively. The challenge is that standard segmentation breaks down mathematically when we look at multiple overlapping attributes. If we pre-specify thousands of possible user cohorts and test them all, we run into the dual problems of sample fragmentation and combinatorial explosion. We lose statistical power, and we drastically inflate our false positive rate.

In this article, we will walk through the architecture and statistical methodology of the Intersectional Monitoring Platform (IMP). This is an end-to-end framework designed to automatically detect, explain, and mitigate intersectional effects in product experimentation without falling into the mathematical traps of traditional segmentation.

## The Origins: Juggernaut and SCOPEX

To understand the design choices behind IMP, it is helpful to look at the two foundational projects that necessitated its creation. Both projects dealt with high-stakes user safety and regulatory compliance, which forced a shift from reactive analytics to systematic, quantitative monitoring.

### Project Juggernaut: Within-Subjects Longitudinal Analysis
Project Juggernaut was a retrospective analysis triggered by regulatory scrutiny. The goal was to understand how users arrived at harmful platform experiences. Because the volume of data was massive (over 300,000 pieces of content per user), manual review was impossible. 

The core methodological innovation of Juggernaut was a "Within-Subjects Tertile Analysis." Instead of comparing different groups of users to each other, the system used each user as their own control. The user's journey was divided into three chronological phases: early, middle, and late platform experience. By tracking metrics like sentiment (via natural language processing) and content diversity (via the Gini coefficient) across these tertiles, the system could detect "intra-individual degradation."

This approach proved that users experiencing harmful outcomes showed predictable trajectories. Their early-phase metrics, which typically showed diverse content consumption and stable sentiment, differed significantly from their late-phase metrics, which showed narrow content focus and declining sentiment.

### Project SCOPEX: The Explainability Mandate
SCOPEX transitioned Juggernaut's retrospective findings into a real-time intervention system. It monitored five universal degradation signals:
1. Content Diversity Narrowing (measured by a daily Gini coefficient)
2. Sentiment Decline (tracking the first derivative of 7-day rolling averages)
3. Policy Violation Acceleration (using hazard rate calculations)
4. Violative Content Exposure
5. Negative Search Behavior

A critical lesson emerged during the implementation of SCOPEX. The engineering team initially built a black-box machine learning model that achieved 94% precision in identifying at-risk users. However, stakeholders rejected it. In high-stakes environments, statistical accuracy is useless if the system cannot explain its flagging decisions. 

**Table 2: Model Trade-off Analysis**

| Model Type | Precision | Recall | Explainability | Stakeholder Approval |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Deep Neural Network (Black-box) | 94.2% | 89.5% | Low (Feature Importance only) | Rejected |
| Logistic Regression (Interpretable) | 91.2% | 87.1% | High (Exact coefficients/odds ratios) | Approved |

The team rebuilt the model using an interpretable logistic regression. While this resulted in a 3% decrease in raw statistical performance, it provided complete transparency. This "explainability mandate" became a core design principle for all future intersectional monitoring.

## The Intersectional Monitoring Platform (IMP)

IMP takes the lessons from Juggernaut and SCOPEX and applies them to everyday product experimentation. It is designed to sit as a supplementary analytical layer on top of existing experimentation stacks like Statsig. It consumes assignment logs and metric tables, runs its intersectional pipeline, and outputs supplementary reports alongside the primary experimental results.

The most important architectural decision in IMP is that it reverses the analytical order of operations. Instead of guessing which user segments might be impacted and testing them all, IMP starts with observed outcomes and works backward.

### Phase 1: Outcome-First Discovery and Validation

This phase is designed to find meaningful user intersections without requiring exhaustive hypothesis pre-specification. It follows a strict four-step statistical process.

**Step 1: Outcome Identification**
The system computes a composite negative outcome score for users in the experiment. This score is based on existing friction metrics like churn, support ticket creation, high undo-command rates, or feature abandonment.

**Step 2: Intersection Analysis**
Next, the system looks at the users with high negative outcome scores and performs a statistical overrepresentation analysis. It scans the constrained user schema to find attribute combinations that appear disproportionately among these struggling users relative to the background distribution. 

**Step 3: Targeted Validation**
Once candidate intersections are identified, the system conducts rigorous treatment-versus-control statistical tests strictly on these specific groups. It uses Welch's t-test, which is crucial here because it does not assume equal variances between the treatment and control groups (a common issue when dealing with small, specific user segments).

**Step 4: Multiple Comparison Control**
Even with targeted validation, testing multiple intersections introduces the risk of Type I errors (false positives). To control for this, IMP applies the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure. This algorithm controls the False Discovery Rate (FDR). It ranks the p-values of all tests, adjusts them based on their rank and the total number of tests, and ensures that the proportion of false discoveries among the rejected null hypotheses remains below a specified threshold (usually 5%). This ensures that when the system flags an intersectional harm, product teams can trust the signal.

*[Interactivity Opportunity: An interactive FDR calculator. Users can input a list of mock p-values and adjust a slider representing the FDR threshold (e.g., 0.01 to 0.10). As the slider moves, a visual list of hypotheses dynamically updates to show which ones are "Rejected" (flagged as true signals) versus "Retained" (flagged as noise).]*

```python
# Implementation of Step 3 and 4: Welch's t-test and Benjamini-Hochberg
import pandas as pd
from scipy import stats
from statsmodels.stats.multitest import multipletests

def validate_intersections(candidate_intersections, treatment_data, control_data, fdr_alpha=0.05):
    results = []
    
    # Step 3: Targeted Validation (Welch's t-test)
    for intersection in candidate_intersections:
        treatment_group = treatment_data[treatment_data['segment'] == intersection]['outcome_score']
        control_group = control_data[control_data['segment'] == intersection]['outcome_score']
        
        # Welch's t-test (equal_var=False)
        t_stat, p_val = stats.ttest_ind(treatment_group, control_group, equal_var=False)
        results.append({'intersection': intersection, 'p_value': p_val, 't_stat': t_stat})
        
    df_results = pd.DataFrame(results)
    
    # Step 4: Multiple Comparison Control (Benjamini-Hochberg)
    reject, pvals_corrected, _, _ = multipletests(df_results['p_value'], alpha=fdr_alpha, method='fdr_bh')
    
    df_results['is_significant'] = reject
    df_results['p_value_adj'] = pvals_corrected
    
    return df_results[df_results['is_significant'] == True]
```

### Phase 2: Predictive Modeling

Once intersections are validated in Phase 1, they are used as labels to train a predictive layer. The goal here is to predict the probability of harm or benefit for new experiments before full data accumulation occurs.

The system featurizes experiments using the intersection attributes (via one-hot encoding) and auto-classified metadata from the experimentation platform (like "AI features" or "onboarding UI"). 

The model of choice is an Elastic Net Logistic Classifier. Elastic Net is particularly well-suited for this task because it combines L1 and L2 regularization. The L1 penalty performs feature selection by shrinking less important interaction coefficients to exactly zero, which makes the final model highly interpretable. The L2 penalty handles correlated features gracefully, which is common when dealing with overlapping user attributes. Class weighting is also applied to handle the inherent imbalance between the majority of normal experiences and the minority of harmful outcomes.

*[2D Visualization Description: A coefficient path plot. The X-axis represents the regularization strength, and the Y-axis represents the coefficient values of various user attributes. As regularization increases, many lines (representing noisy features) drop exactly to zero (due to L1), while a few key lines (representing true intersectional drivers) remain non-zero, demonstrating the feature selection capability of Elastic Net.]*

```python
# Implementation of Phase 2: Elastic Net Logistic Classifier
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.pipeline import Pipeline

def train_predictive_model(X_train, y_train):
    # X_train contains one-hot encoded intersection attributes and metadata
    # y_train contains binary labels of validated harm (1) or normal (0)
    
    pipeline = Pipeline([
        ('scaler', StandardScaler()),
        # Elastic Net configuration: penalty='elasticnet', solver='saga'
        # l1_ratio=0.5 balances L1 (Lasso) and L2 (Ridge) penalties
        # class_weight='balanced' handles the minority harmful outcomes
        ('classifier', LogisticRegression(
            penalty='elasticnet',
            l1_ratio=0.5,
            solver='saga',
            class_weight='balanced',
            max_iter=1000
        ))
    ])
    
    pipeline.fit(X_train, y_train)
    return pipeline
```

### Phases 3 and 4: Continuous Monitoring and Simulation

IMP extends beyond active experiments. In Phase 3, it performs platform-wide regression monitoring. It streams events and computes z-scores for error rates across high-volume intersections. To minimize alert fatigue, the trigger threshold is set at a strict 7-sigma (seven standard deviations). When an alert fires, it provides a quantified segment definition that engineers can immediately use for reproduction.

Phase 4 provides a simulation sandbox. Product teams can input draft hypotheses and parameters before writing any code. The trained models predict effect sizes and provide bootstrap intervals for uncertainty, allowing teams to design responsible rollout stages and determine required sample sizes in advance.

## Schema Design and Privacy-Preserving Proxies

To make this math work at scale without combinatorial explosion, the data schema must be intentionally constrained. IMP restricts user personas to 19 carefully selected variables across three types:
* Categorical: Plan type, region, OS, journey stage.
* Continuous: Tenure, team size, session frequency, undo rates.
* Binary: Opt-ins, new user flags, solo user flags.

**Table 3: Constrained User Schema Examples**

| Variable Type | Attribute Name | Allowed Values / Range |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |

By limiting the schema, the system can compute multi-way interactions (for example, New User * LATAM Region * Solo User) efficiently.

### The Accessibility Likelihood Materialization
One of the most elegant technical solutions in IMP is how it handles accessibility metrics. Directly fingerprinting assistive technology raises significant privacy and ethical concerns. Instead, IMP uses a transparent, behavioral proxy score.

A daily PySpark job aggregates UI events over a 30-day rolling window to identify heuristics indicative of assistive technology use. These include:
* The percentage of focus events on non-interactive elements (like paragraphs or divs).
* The frequency of navigation keyboard events per minute (Tab, Arrow keys).
* The usage of skip links and ARIA widgets.

These raw behavioral metrics are converted to Z-scores relative to platform cohorts. The system then applies a weighted scoring logic. For example, an explicit accessibility opt-in might be worth 4.0 points, while a positive Z-score for keyboard navigation might be worth 1.5 points. 

Finally, this sum is passed through a sigmoid function to map the unbounded score into a clean 0.0 to 1.0 probability range. Users exceeding a 0.6 threshold are flagged for the intersectional analysis. This transforms accessibility measurement from an invisible blind spot into a systematic, privacy-preserving metric.

```python
# PySpark Implementation of Accessibility Likelihood Materialization
import pyspark.sql.functions as F
from pyspark.sql.types import DoubleType
import math

# UDF for Sigmoid function
def sigmoid(x):
    return 1 / (1 + math.exp(-x))
sigmoid_udf = F.udf(sigmoid, DoubleType())

def calculate_accessibility_proxy(df_events):
    # Assume df_events contains z_scores for behaviors and opt_in flags
    df_scored = df_events.withColumn(
        "raw_score",
        (F.col("opt_in_flag") * 4.0) + 
        (F.when(F.col("z_score_keyboard_nav") > 0, F.col("z_score_keyboard_nav") * 1.5).otherwise(0)) +
        (F.when(F.col("z_score_aria_usage") > 0, F.col("z_score_aria_usage") * 2.0).otherwise(0))
    )
    
    # Apply sigmoid to map to 0.0 - 1.0 probability range
    df_probabilities = df_scored.withColumn(
        "accessibility_likelihood", 
        sigmoid_udf(F.col("raw_score"))
    )
    
    # Flag users exceeding the 0.6 threshold
    return df_probabilities.withColumn(
        "is_a11y_proxy", 
        F.when(F.col("accessibility_likelihood") > 0.6, True).otherwise(False)
    )
```

## System Architecture and Governance

The entire framework is built on an AWS-native Medallion data architecture. 
* The Bronze layer stores raw UI events, session metrics, and experiment assignments in S3.
* The Silver layer uses PySpark jobs to normalize this data into daily snapshots, joining exposure data with the generated user personas.
* The Gold layer runs the Python-based intersectional modeling (via SageMaker or AWS Batch) and outputs the FDR-adjusted findings and risk registries.

*[3D Visualization Description: An isometric architectural diagram. Three distinct storage tiers (Bronze, Silver, Gold) are represented as cylindrical databases. Data flows from user devices into the Bronze tier. Spark clusters (represented as interconnected nodes) process data from Bronze to Silver. Finally, a machine learning node processes Silver data into the Gold tier, which then branches out into report documents and alert icons.]*

Governance is built directly into the pipeline. The system enforces k-anonymity by automatically suppressing any data cell where the sample size is less than 200 users, preventing any possibility of re-identification. Furthermore, persona generation is strictly gated by user consent flags at the row level.

```python
# Governance Implementation: k-anonymity enforcement
def enforce_k_anonymity(df_intersections, k_threshold=200):
    # Filter out any intersectional group with fewer than 'k' users
    compliant_df = df_intersections[df_intersections['user_count'] >= k_threshold]
    
    # Log suppressed segments for auditing without revealing user data
    suppressed_count = len(df_intersections) - len(compliant_df)
    print(f"Governance Alert: Suppressed {suppressed_count} segments failing k-anonymity (k={k_threshold})")
    
    return compliant_df
```

Finally, AWS Lambda and Jinja2 templates automatically compile the Gold layer findings into plain-language HTML and PDF reports, delivering prioritized recommendations directly to product teams via Slack.

## Conclusion

The historical application of IMP has consistently revealed three critical blind spots in traditional A/B testing:
1. Hidden Harm: Global metrics that mask severe negative impacts on specific intersectional groups.
2. Missed Opportunities: Features that appear neutral globally but drive massive engagement for specific, overlapping demographics.
3. Invisible Impacts: Algorithmic updates that negatively affect specific behavioral patterns.

By shifting from reactive analytics to a proactive, outcome-first statistical framework, we can stop optimizing for the mythical average user. Granular intersectional monitoring allows us to turn the abstract principle of inclusive design into a measurable, automated engineering practice.
