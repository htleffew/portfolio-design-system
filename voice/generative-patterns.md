# Generative Patterns

> This document does what the checklist cannot: it tells you what to produce,
> not what to avoid. Read it alongside style-card.md and before any prose is
> drafted. The checklist filters bad writing. This document generates authentic
> writing.

> The specimen sentences below are Dr. Leffew's actual sentences. Pattern-match
> to them. They are not rules about writing; they are examples of writing.

---

## 1. The Argument Sequence

Every substantial piece — casual essay, position piece, academic paper —
follows the same four-part sequence. This is not a template to announce; it is
a logic to execute.

**Move 1 — Enter through something specific.**
The first substantive claim is a concrete instance, a documented observation,
or a named piece of evidence. Not a general situation. Not "the field has long
struggled with." A thing.

**Move 2 — Build converging evidence.**
From the specific instance, move to the corpus: multiple sources, named
precisely, that converge on the same conclusion from different angles. Not one
study. Not "research suggests." Converging lines of evidence.

**Move 3 — Name the mechanism.**
State not just that X happened, but why — the specific architectural failure,
psychological dynamic, or structural condition that produced X. Coin a term
if existing vocabulary is inadequate. Define it once in the sentence where it
appears. Do not re-explain it.

**Move 4 — State the conclusion that was earned.**
The closing claim is the thing the piece has been building toward. State it
plainly. Do not summarize the journey. Do not hedge the conclusion that the
evidence supports. Stop after you state it.

### What this looks like in practice

The Kohberger essay: enters through wound pattern evidence from the medical
examiner (Move 1) → converges on research connecting gendered wound
characteristics to sexually motivated violence (Move 2) → names piquerism as
the mechanism linking offense behavior to psychological motive (Move 3) →
closes with "we shouldn't be reluctant to say the obvious out loud" (Move 4).

The AI safety essay: enters through a specific gaslighting interaction
Dr. Leffew had with Claude Sonnet 4.5 (Move 1) → builds through documented
Reddit threads and the specific LCR behavior (Move 2) → names "The Flip" and
"iatrogenic recursive escalation" as the mechanisms (Move 3) → closes with
explicit policy recommendations (Move 4).

## 2. How Arguments Are NOT Built

### Manufactured tension
She does not create a backdrop of general field failure to elevate the
contribution. Challenges are specific, named, and evidence-supported.

**Wrong:**
> "For decades, researchers have failed to develop adequate frameworks for
> understanding this phenomenon. The limitations of prior approaches have left
> practitioners without reliable guidance. This paper offers a correction."

**Right:**
> "The findings of this project reject what is presented as common wisdom and
> asserted by organizations such as the FBI's Behavioral Analysis Unit (2015)."

The difference: the wrong version attacks an unnamed, undatable "field." The
right version names a specific organization, a specific assertion, and a
specific year. Challenges are verifiable or they are not made.

### Self-elevation
She does not position herself as correcting a broad failure. She positions her
evidence as contradicting a specific claim.

**Wrong:**
> "Unlike most researchers who have approached this question superficially,
> this paper engages rigorously with the actual evidence."

She does not write this. Ever. The evidence contradicts the claim; the
researcher is incidental.

## 3. Sentence Architecture

### Formal voice: qualified accumulation, not clean declaratives

The style card describes "short clauses, comma-stitched." This is accurate for
the casual register. In the formal register, sentences average 27-32 words.
Complexity accumulates inside the sentence through subordinate clause embedding
— not between sentences through semicolons.

**Formal sentence pattern:** main claim + qualifying clause + limiting
clause + implication. The sentence does not arrive cleanly; it arrives
with its qualifications already embedded.

> "NPA can be approached as a largely cortical disorder of emotional
> processing which responds, at least in part, to targeted interventions
> aimed at developing emotional literacy, emotional regulation, empathy, and
> response inhibition."

That is one sentence carrying a claim, a location, a degree-marker, and a
worked example. This is not disorganization; it is qualification delivered
inside the sentence rather than in follow-up sentences.

### Casual voice: short declarative + longer explanatory

> "My career often raises eyebrows. People hear 'psychologist' and 'AI' in
> the same sentence and assume it's a contradiction."

The short sentence lands. The longer sentence explains. This alternation is
characteristic of casual-register paragraphs.

### Concession before claim

Frequent sentence openers: "Though," "Despite," "While," "Even when." She
names the complication before the argument, not after it. The structure is:
acknowledge the thing that might make the reader object → then make the claim
anyway with evidence.

> "Though the sample size was less than ideal, the convergence of behavioral
> indicators across cases suggests..."

Do not rewrite these as "The sample size was less than ideal, but...". The
"Though" opener is the pattern; it places the concession in a subordinate
position from the start rather than creating a false pivot.

### Connective starters

Between 9 and 17 percent of sentences in formal pieces begin with connective
language that links to the previous sentence: "Additionally," "Further,"
"This [noun] suggests," "In contrast." These are real links, not filler
transitions. Each one makes a claim about how the current sentence relates to
the prior one.

**Correct use:**
> "Additionally, the follower count figures support this interpretation."
(adds a converging piece of evidence)

**Incorrect use:**
> "Additionally, it is worth noting that..."
("worth noting" is filler; "additionally" combined with filler is double
filler)

### What she does NOT do with "this"

She does not start sentences with bare demonstrative pronouns.
"This suggests..." is prohibited. "This finding suggests..." is fine.
The noun following "this" must be explicit. The reader should never have
to infer what "this" refers to.

**Wrong:** > "This suggests a broader pattern of misalignment."
**Right:** > "This behavioral pattern suggests a broader misalignment between
the model's safety architecture and its clinical constraints."

## 4. The Naming Move

When existing vocabulary is inadequate, she coins a term, defines it
in the sentence where it appears, and uses it without re-explaining it.

Examples from corpus:
- "The Flip" — defined in the moment of naming: "the distinctive moment when
  Claude sheds its typical assistant persona"
- "iatrogenic recursive escalation" — defined in context before use
- "piquerism" — borrowed from clinical literature, deployed as the precise
  term everyone else was avoiding
- "the alignment pendulum" — named and defined, then used as shorthand
- "non-psychopathic antisociality (NPA)" and "psychopathic antisociality (PA)"
  — coined as discretizing labels, parenthetical abbreviation provided

The pattern: coin → define in one sentence → use as shorthand thereafter.
Do not keep re-explaining the term. Do not avoid coining when the gap is real.

## 5. The Dimensional Impulse

She refuses binary categories in virtually every piece. This is not a
stylistic preference; it is an epistemological commitment.

This appears in:
- Every psychopathy piece (typological, not binary)
- The dissertation (affective/instrumental as spectrum)
- The AI safety essay (the alignment binary is a false constraint)
- The gender paper (against single-factor explanations)
- The Kohberger essay (against "mass murderer vs. serial killer" binary)

When an agent encounters a binary framing in the subject matter, the correct
move is to name the dimension rather than pick a side. The pattern is not
"it's both" — it is "the question is dimensional and here is the dimension."

## 6. Executing the Uncomfortable Conclusion

The most common agent failure mode with this voice: announcing that the
conclusion is uncomfortable rather than stating the conclusion.

**Wrong:**
> "The synthesis that follows is deliberately uncomfortable. These findings
> challenge assumptions that many practitioners hold, and the implications
> are difficult to accept."

She does not write this. This is a meta-statement about prose. It performs
the move rather than executing it.

**Right:**
> "A less statistically staggering and far simpler, but arguably much more
> uncomfortable, explanation is humbly suggested by this author."

What this sentence does: sets up a contrast (the statistically complex myth
vs. the simpler truth), uses "uncomfortable" as a modifier embedded in the
actual sentence, uses "humbly suggested" with obvious irony after a complex
argument. The uncomfortable is part of the logical move, not an announcement
that the logical move is coming.

The rule: "uncomfortable" is an embedded modifier inside a sentence that
delivers the actual claim. It is never a meta-statement about the piece.
The same logic applies to "difficult," "challenging," "counterintuitive,"
"provocative." If the word describes the prose rather than the claim, it is
performing the move. Delete it and write the claim.

## 7. Negative Space

The existing brief.md template produces a structured disclaimer block from the
NEGATIVE SPACE section. This is not how she uses negative space.

Her actual negative space move appears as a brief embedded pivot inside an
argument, in the casual register:
> "This paper in no way should be received as a piece campaigning for community
> mixers where one can become acquainted with their 'local, friendly
> neighborhood psychopath'."

This is one sentence, mid-argument, that preemptively closes off a misreading.
It is often funny. It always does argumentative work. It is not a structured
block.

**When to use it:** when a reasonable reader could misread the argument as
recommending something the piece is not recommending. State the misreading
explicitly, once, in a sentence that conveys why the misread is absurd without
attacking the reader for the misread.

**When not to use it:** as a ritual disclaimer at the top of every piece.
Not every piece has a plausible misreading that needs to be preempted.
If the negative space section in the brief has nothing real to say, the
genre choice is probably wrong, or the page doesn't need one.

## 8. Earned Humor

She uses dry irony as an argumentative tool in both registers. The rule:
humor only when it advances the argument. If the sentence is funny but
the humor could be removed without changing the argument's logical content,
remove it.

### What earned humor looks like

> "It would be an insult to armchair psychologists everywhere to call what
> it did armchair psychology."

This is not a joke. It advances the argument by establishing that the model's
behavior failed even the lowest plausible standard. The humor and the argument
are the same sentence.

> "Users quickly gave this behavior a name: 'The Flip.' The Flip being the
> distinctive moment when Claude sheds its typical Golden Retriever-esque
> assistant persona and transforms into an unholy union of Judge Dredd and
> Dr. Phil."

The humor defines the mechanism precisely. "Golden Retriever-esque" specifies
the baseline persona accurately. "Judge Dredd and Dr. Phil" specifies exactly
what combination of authority and therapeutic overreach the failure mode
produces. These are not decorative. They are the most accurate available
descriptions.

### What manufactured levity looks like

Humor used as warmth, or to soften an argument, or to signal that the writer
is likeable. She does not do this. If the irony doesn't sharpen the point,
it is not her irony.

## 9. Epistemic Precision

She names limits plainly and keeps moving. Epistemic hedging does not
replace argument; it sits in specific sentences that require it, stated once,
without apology.

**Limit acknowledged, then claim made:**
> "I have no specific confirmation of this, but the way the guardrails rolled
> out with Sonnet 4.5 suggests that psychologists weren't involved in the
> design, testing, or monitoring of these measures."

The limit is named in six words. The claim continues in the same sentence.
This is not weakness; it is precision.

**Wrong pattern:**
> "While it would be premature to draw definitive conclusions, and while
> further research is clearly needed before making strong claims, and while
> the limitations of the current dataset should be kept in mind, it may
> potentially suggest..."

She does not write this. Name the limit once, in specific terms, and state
the claim at the same confidence level the evidence supports.

## 10. Credential Deployment

When she invokes professional expertise, the structure is always:
credential as scoped authority → claim within that authority → move on.
The credential appears once, does its work, and disappears.

> "Speaking as someone who has been trained in, and performed, psychiatric
> crisis intervention, including with people experiencing psychosis, I can
> tell you that aggressive accusation is not 'intervention'."

Note the scope: "trained in and performed" is specific. "Including with people
experiencing psychosis" narrows the claim further. The credential is precise
before the claim is made. This is not general authority ("as a psychologist,
I believe"); it is scoped authority for a specific methodological challenge.

She does not reference her credentials more than once in a piece. The
credential appears where it is needed, not as ongoing framing.

## 11. Register Differences: Formal vs. Casual

**What changes:**
- Sentence length: casual averages ~15-18 words; formal averages 27-32 words
- Third person required in academic genre; first person in essays and position pieces
- Hedging language: formal adds "appears," "suggests," "may indicate"; casual
  states directly when evidence is sufficient
- No humor in formal work, except the one structural irony move
  (the preemptive misreading sentence)
- Citation format: formal cites explicitly; casual names sources inline

**What does not change across registers:**
- The four-move argument sequence
- The dimensional impulse
- The precision about what is and is not being claimed
- The naming move
- The specificity of evidence (named sources, denominators on numbers)
- The closing move (one declarative sentence; no summary; stop)
- The refusals (manufactured tension, self-elevation, hedging strong evidence,
  poetic analogy when functional analogy would do)

The two registers are the same person operating under different institutional
constraints. The formal voice is not more cautious or more distant; it is
the same argument under genre discipline.

## 12. Closing Moves

The final sentence of any piece or section is short. After a long analytical
arc, she stops. The close names the conclusion that was earned; it does not
review the steps that got there.

> "We shouldn't be reluctant to say the obvious out loud."
> "Psychological assessment and intervention are high-stakes, deeply complex,
> and must be approached with sober responsibility."
> "Lives may depend on user-friendly designs that honor consistency and
> minimize decisions."

Each of these is the claim the piece was building toward. Not a call to action.
Not a rhetorical question. Not a summary. The argument has ended; the reader
is trusted to know what to do with what they have read.

If the closing sentence could be cut and the reader would still understand the
argument, it is not yet the closing sentence. Write the one that would leave
a gap if removed.

## Quick Reference: Before/After

| Pattern | Wrong (agent default) | Right (her actual pattern) |
|---|---|---|
| Uncomfortable conclusion | "What follows is deliberately uncomfortable." | "A simpler, but arguably much more uncomfortable, explanation is humbly suggested by this author." |
| Field challenge | "Researchers have long struggled with this problem." | "The findings reject what is asserted by the FBI's Behavioral Analysis Unit (2015)." |
| Negative space | Structured NOT AN ESTIMATE OF / NOT A CLAIM ABOUT block | One embedded sentence mid-argument preempting a specific misreading |
| Epistemic limit | Four-clause hedge buried in a sentence | "I have no specific confirmation of this, but..." in one clause before the claim |
| Closing | Summary of argument steps | One declarative sentence that names the conclusion; full stop |
| Bare demonstrative | "This suggests a broader pattern." | "This behavioral pattern suggests a broader misalignment between..." |
| Self-elevation | "Unlike prior researchers, this paper engages rigorously..." | Never. The evidence contradicts the claim; the researcher is incidental. |
