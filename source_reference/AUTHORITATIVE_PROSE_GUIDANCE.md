# Portfolio: Authoritative Prose & Writing Guide

> This document defines the overarching rules for authoring any text, project, or component
> within the portfolio. It is a companion to `design_system/VOICE.md` and the `voice/`
> subdirectory. Where documents conflict, `voice/generative-patterns.md` and
> `voice/style-card.md` take precedence as the most current and corpus-validated sources.

---

## 1. The Core Frame: Argument from Evidence

The portfolio exists to make a case, not to demonstrate expertise. Each piece — case study,
methodology essay, position piece — advances a specific argument supported by specific
evidence. The argument was formed before the writing began. The prose builds the case for it.

The reader is a peer: intelligent, capable, intellectually curious, but not assumed to share
the author's exact specialization. This means technical terms are used precisely and defined
once on first reference — not translated into approximations. "Gradient boosted trees" is
the right term; "a machine learning method that improves predictions step by step" is not.
The definition follows the term in the same sentence or the next one, and then the term is
used plainly for the rest of the piece.

The posture is not mentorship. It is not warm and patient explanation. It is a peer
presenting reasoning that the reader is fully capable of following. The difference matters
in practice: mentorship scaffolds; argument advances. Scaffolding produces prose that
explains what it is about to do, summarizes what it just did, and checks whether the reader
is keeping up. Argument produces prose that makes the next move.

## 2. Tone and Register

The overarching tone is precise, direct, and peer-to-peer. Not cold. Not detached. But not
warm in the sense of solicitous or encouraging. The prose trusts the reader to follow and
does not perform that trust by saying so.

**Technical terms with definitions, not substitutions.** When clinical, statistical, or
technical vocabulary is the right vocabulary, use it. Pair it with a definition the first
time it appears. Do not replace "isotonic calibration" with "a method that adjusts the
model's confidence" — explain what isotonic calibration does and why it was chosen, then
use the term. The precise term, defined once, is a courtesy; repeated circumlocution is
condescension.

**Functional analogies where they name a concept, not where they soften one.** Analogies
are permitted when they are mechanical and give the reader a concept they can carry
forward: "a two-pass filter," "a gating mechanism," "a soft-voting ensemble." Analogies
are not permitted when their purpose is to make a difficult idea seem simpler than it is,
or when removing the analogy would leave the technical content incomplete. The math is the
proof. The analogy is a handle on the math, not a replacement for it.

**No manufacturing of tension.** Challenges are to specific named claims with specific
verifiable evidence. The prose does not create a backdrop of general field failure to
elevate the contribution. It does not position the author as correcting what everyone else
has gotten wrong. It positions the evidence as contradicting a specific claim.

**Earned irony, not performed warmth.** Dry humor appears when it does argumentative work:
when the ironic framing is the most precise available description of the thing. It does not
appear as a softening device or a signal that the author is approachable.

## 3. Strict Syntactic and Structural Guidelines

**Universal ban on em dashes (—) in prose.** Use commas, semicolons, colons, or new
sentences. Hyphens for compound modifiers are correct.

**No parallel contrarian structures.** Never "Not X, but Y" or "Less X, more Y." Make the
positive claim directly.

**No manufactured tension.** "The question was never X" — just answer the operative
question.

**No bare demonstrative openers.** "This suggests..." is wrong. "This finding suggests..."
is correct. The noun following "This" must be explicit.

**No subjunctive for completed work.** "The architecture I would build" is wrong when the
architecture was built. "The architecture I built" is correct.

**Banned vocabulary** (run a literal search before submitting any draft):
ecosystem, journey, space (in the AI space), passionate about, results-driven,
proven track record, leverage/utilize (use "use"), unlock, empower, transform,
cutting-edge, revolutionary, groundbreaking, pioneering, thought leader, seamless,
robust, powerful, solution, tapestry, symphony, orchestra, dance, weaving,
industry-leading, tactical velocity, executive telemetry.

## 4. Argument Architecture

Every substantial piece follows this logic, not necessarily this structure:

1. **Enter through something specific.** A named piece of evidence, a documented
   observation, a concrete instance. Not "the field has long struggled with."
2. **Build converging evidence.** Multiple named sources from different angles. Not one
   study paraphrased into a general claim.
3. **Name the mechanism.** Not just what happened, but why, in specific terms. Coin a
   term if existing vocabulary is inadequate; define it once and use it.
4. **State the conclusion the evidence earned.** Plainly. Sized to what the evidence
   supports. Do not hedge conclusions the evidence supports; do not assert conclusions
   it does not.

## 5. Closings

The final sentence of any piece or major section is short. It names the conclusion that
was earned. It does not summarize the journey. It does not call to action. It does not
congratulate the reader for following along. The argument has ended; the reader is trusted
to know what to do with it.

If the final sentence could be removed without leaving a gap, it is not yet the final
sentence. Write the one that would leave a gap if removed.
