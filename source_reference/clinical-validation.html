Multimodal clinical AI validation with surfaced uncertainty

A late-fusion multimodal pipeline with per-modality Platt and isotonic calibration produces clinical reports where the algorithm operates strictly as an assistant.

Problem Space

Identifying the clinical measurement variance

In real clinical settings, autism isn't a simple binary. Our synthetic data shows that 60 percent to 80 percent of behavioral signals overlap perfectly between ASD and non-ASD children. When you add in the natural noise of a restless toddler in an assessment room, a simple binary machine learning classification completely fails. It's just too brittle.

If an AI simply spits out a '0.74 probability of autism', that's useless to a clinician. It strips away all the context. Did the model see poor eye contact, or repetitive hand flapping? Did those signals contradict each other? To fix this, our pipeline outputs a structured evidence summary. It forces the system to quantify exactly which behaviors are present and how far they deviate from the norm, mapping directly to standard clinical observation rubrics.

Behavioral Architecture

Isolated feature domains

To achieve this, we split the architecture into separate, independent modeling streams. We evaluate Social Engagement by tracking continuous variables like gaze-to-face percentage and mutual gaze. We evaluate Communication by isolating how long it takes a child to respond to their name. And we evaluate Restricted Behavior by tracking how much time is spent in functional play versus repetitive motion loops.

Why build separate streams? Because forcing all of these signals into a single, massive neural network flattens everything into a black box. By keeping each domain in its own isolated execution environment, we preserve the specific behavioral vocabularies needed for accurate clinical documentation.

Architecture

Late fusion ensemble structures

Under the hood, we deploy dedicated XGBoost models across each of these behavioral subsets. This gives us three distinct probability risk layers: one for social, one for communication, and one for repetitive behaviors. Finally, a secondary 'meta-learner' takes these three independent probabilities and intelligently fuses them together to form a final prediction.

This 'late-fusion' structure allows us to explicitly capture algorithmic disagreement. If the model sees severe social and repetitive symptoms, but completely typical communication skills, the meta-learner immediately flags the divergence. This triggers an anomaly alert on the dashboard, strictly requiring a human expert to review the complicated case.

Calibration

Non-parametric monotone calibration

Standard gradient-boosted models have a major flaw: they are wildly overconfident. Their raw outputs get mathematically squashed toward 0 percent or 100 percent, completely misrepresenting the true uncertainty of a prediction. For a low-stakes app, that's fine. But in a clinical setting, where probabilities communicate medical evidence, an overconfident model is incredibly dangerous.

To fix this, we have to formally calibrate the probabilities. We use Platt scaling to map the raw scores onto a smooth logistic curve, and isotonic regression to strictly enforce that higher model scores always correspond to legitimately higher diagnostic risk. This ensures our percentages actually reflect reality, without forcing the data into unnatural mathematical assumptions.

Crucially, we calibrate each behavioral stream independently. The baseline distributions for communication delays look very different from the baseline distributions of repetitive behaviors. If we tried to apply one giant, unified correction across all models simultaneously, it would mathematically corrupt the entire landscape.

Uncertainty Surfacing

Explicit uncertainty surfacing

To quantify uncertainty, the pipeline calculates the standard deviation across the primary estimators. If the models vehemently disagree with one another (a standard deviation above 0.20), the system literally halts. It refuses to output a falsely confident prediction, and instead outputs the variance itself as the clinical conclusion, triggering an expert review.

The user interface translates this math directly into diagnostic confidence. Standard deviations below 0.10 return 'High Confidence', while anything above 0.20 triggers a 'Low Confidence' warning. When an anomaly is flagged, the clinical report highlights exactly which behavioral features triggered the confusion, pinpointing the metrics that deviated the furthest from the norm.

Conversely, features that perfectly match typical neurodevelopmental baselines are compiled as positive counterpoints. Ultimately, the model doesn't make a diagnosis. It simply synthesizes the noisy, contradictory evidence into a mathematically sound framework, allowing the human clinician to make the final call.

Architecture Principles

Core architectural directives

01
Domain execution isolation
Separate mathematical feature distributions into independent models. Forcing all parameters into a unified classification gradient deletes domain-specific context.
02
Late fusion hierarchy
Process independent probability arrays through a meta-learner interface. Connecting features before model evaluation eliminates the capacity to determine predictive origin.
03
Discrete modality calibration
Apply Platt and isotonic scalers uniquely per stream. Feature subsets maintain mutually exclusive class imbalances requiring independent corrective margins.
04
Deterministic uncertainty proxies
Output inter-model disagreement explicitly. Suppressing deviation logic in favor of mean averages deletes critical outcome limitations.
05
Evidence structuring logic
The primary artifact organizes evidence rather than generating fixed diagnostic percentages. The clinical protocol incorporates numerical indices alongside direct behavioral mapping.
06
Diagnostic measurement mapping
Force mathematical indicators to bind specifically to real-world validation endpoints. Model limits must connect cleanly to established field schedules.
